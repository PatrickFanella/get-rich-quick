# Value Investing

Buys securities trading below intrinsic worth (low P/B, P/E, P/CF). Codified by Graham & Dodd in _Security Analysis_ (1934) with the concept of "margin of safety."

## Key Evidence

- **Fama & French (1992, JF 47(2), 427-465)**: Book-to-market captures cross-sectional return variation far better than CAPM beta. Directly challenged the Capital Asset Pricing Model.
- **Lakonishok, Shleifer & Vishny (1994, JF 49(5), 1541-1578)**: Investors systematically extrapolate past growth rates, overpaying for "glamour" stocks. Value stocks did not underperform during downturns, undermining the risk explanation.
- **Basu (1977)**: Early evidence that low P/E stocks outperform high P/E stocks.

## The Value Premium Decline

The value premium has faced serious questions since the late 2000s:

- Fama & French (2021, RAPS) acknowledged second-half premiums were "on average much lower"
- Primary explanation: **book equity is a poor proxy** as intangible assets have grown
- Goncalves & Stulz (2023, JFE) showed fundamental-to-market ratio largely explains the apparent decline
- Outside the US, the premium has persisted more robustly
- Composite value measures adjusting for intangibles still show meaningful effects

## The Debate

See [[risk-vs-mispricing-debate]]:

- **Risk explanation** (Fama & French): Value stocks are distressed, riskier firms; premium compensates for bearing that risk
- **Behavioral explanation** (Lakonishok et al.): Investors overextrapolate past growth, systematically mispricing

## Performance

- Historical premium: ~4-6% per year
- Sharpe ratio: ~0.3-0.5 over decades
- Low turnover (annual rebalancing)
