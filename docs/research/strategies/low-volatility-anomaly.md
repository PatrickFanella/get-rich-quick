# Low-Volatility Anomaly

Perhaps the most theoretically puzzling finding in empirical finance. CAPM predicts higher risk (beta) earns higher returns, but the empirical Security Market Line is dramatically flatter than theory predicts.

## Key Evidence

- **Black, Jensen & Scholes (1972)**: First documented the flat empirical SML
- **Ang, Hodrick, Xing & Zhang (2006, JF 61(1), 259-299)**: Stocks with high idiosyncratic volatility earn "abysmally low" average returns, unexplained by size, value, momentum, or liquidity
- **Baker, Bradley & Wurgler (2011, FAJ)**: Called this "the greatest anomaly in finance"

## Betting Against Beta (BAB)

**Frazzini & Pedersen (2014, JFE 111(1), 1-25)**: The leading theoretical explanation.

When investors face **leverage constraints** (margin requirements, regulatory limits):

1. They cannot simply leverage low-beta assets
2. Instead, they tilt toward high-beta stocks to reach desired return targets
3. This bids up high-beta prices, compressing their future returns

BAB factor produces significant risk-adjusted returns across:

- US equities
- 20 international equity markets
- Treasury bonds, corporate bonds, and futures

## Complementary Explanation

Institutional investors benchmarked to cap-weighted indices are penalized for holding low-beta stocks (creates tracking error), effectively preventing arbitrage of the anomaly.

## Limitations

- Underperforms in strong bull markets
- Interest rate sensitivity from sector tilts toward utilities and consumer staples
- Risk-adjusted outperformance over full market cycles is among the most well-documented effects
