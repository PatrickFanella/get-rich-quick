# LLM-Powered Trading Bots: Architecture and Data Inputs  
**LLMs in trading** serve as *agents* that process diverse data (news, reports, social media, price feeds) into trade signals. They can **directly generate buy/sell decisions** or act as “alpha miners” that output features or factors for a classic algo model【21†L112-L121】【9†L75-L83】. Modern systems might fuse an LLM-driven sentiment or news model with a price forecaster (e.g. an LSTM) to generate trades【9†L75-L83】. For example, an adversarial-trading study built an ATS in Backtrader that combines LLM-based news sentiment (FinBERT/FinGPT/etc.) with an LSTM price model【9†L75-L83】. Compared to humans, LLMs excel at quickly ingesting text and summarizing insights – a key advantage since traders must act on fresh information rapidly【9†L87-L94】【21†L34-L43】.

Popular architectures include **“news-driven” agents** (LLM ingests news/macro data to predict next-period moves【21†L119-L126】), **“reflection”-driven agents** (LLM rephrases or chains reasoning about past performance), **“debate”-driven systems** (multiple LLMs argue over a decision), and **reinforcement-learning agents** (LLM policies fine-tuned via reward)【21†L112-L121】【51†L37-L45】. Other LLM-based tools act as **alpha miners**, e.g. generating technical factors or summarizing fundamentals for downstream models【21†L112-L121】. In all cases, the LLM must be fed timely data: for stocks this means corporate filings, news wires, and price feeds; for crypto, blockchain data and social channels; for prediction markets, event data and oracle feeds.  

## Strategy Design and Signal Generation  
**Traditional algo strategies** (momentum, mean-reversion, arbitrage, market-making, statistical patterns) remain core. LLMs can **augment strategy design** by, for example, generating candidate patterns or natural-language rules from data. More concretely, LLMs excel at **sentiment- and news-based strategies**: they can analyze headlines, tweets or financial reports and translate them into buy/sell signals【9†L87-L94】【51†L37-L45】. In stock trading, an LLM might be prompted to rate the sentiment of an earnings call transcript or analyst report; in crypto, it could judge social sentiment or news tone. Research shows fine-tuning this process (e.g. having separate LLM modules for “factual” vs “subjective” reasoning) can improve crypto performance【51†L37-L45】.  

- *News-driven sentiment:* Feed LLM financial news and ask for short-term price direction. E.g. a system might use FinBERT/FinGPT/ChatGPT to gauge sentiment on daily news【9†L87-L94】.  
- *Rule generation:* Ask the LLM to propose trading rules (e.g. “buy X if RSI < 30”), then backtest those rules. The QuantInsti guide shows using prompts to generate Python backtests for mean-reversion or momentum strategies【42†L13-L22】【35†L35-L42】.  
- *Multi-agent reasoning:* Advanced bots use multiple LLM roles. For example, an FS-ReasoningAgent framework ran a “factual” LLM and a “subjective” LLM in parallel to analyze crypto news; this fine-grained approach yielded 7–10% higher profits than simpler LLM pipelines【51†L37-L45】.  

Polymarket (prediction market) bots focus on binary event odds. Example strategies from practitioners include:  
- **Last-second directional bets:** Buy “yes” or “no” in the final seconds if the price is under a threshold【5†L67-L75】. Itan Scott’s Polymarket bot would place fixed-size bets when a 5-min contract price was still low (e.g. 0.85–0.99)【5†L67-L75】.  
- **Oracle checks:** Combine LLM/chain analysis (e.g. LLM oracles check) to verify market odds. One Polymarket bot used a Chainlink oracle to ensure the price feed was accurate before trading【5†L72-L75】.  
- **Market-making:** Quote both sides of the order book around mid-price (like traditional AMM market-making). Scott’s Bot 3 automatically placed simultaneous buy/sell on the Polymarket orderbook to capture spreads【5†L78-L83】.  

For **crypto markets**, strategies often mix technical rules with LLM-backed news analysis. You might program an LLM to forecast price moves given on-chain data (like wallet flows) and trend indicators. Or build multi-agent chains (as FS-Reasoning did) to adapt strategy by market regime (bull vs bear)【51†L37-L45】. In all cases, strategies should be tested historically (see later).  

## Order Creation and Execution  
After selecting a strategy, the bot must issue orders via market APIs. Key points:  
- **Order types:** Common orders include *market orders* (execute immediately), *limit orders* (set a target price), and *conditional orders* (stop-loss or take-profit). In stocks and crypto, you also have *stop-limit* (trigger a limit order at a stop price). Choosing order types affects execution risk – e.g. market orders guarantee fill but may “slip” in volatile markets【35†L59-L67】. Your bot’s trading logic should decide which to use (e.g. market orders for urgent exit, limit orders for precision).  
- **Market interfaces:**  
  - *Stocks:* Use broker APIs (e.g. Alpaca, Interactive Brokers, Tradier) or trading libraries. Alpaca and Backtrader are popular for U.S. equities【38†L9-L11】. These let your Python bot place trades and fetch data.  
  - *Crypto:* Use exchange APIs via libraries like CCXT. This supports Binance, Coinbase, Kraken, etc., for both REST and WebSocket feeds. You might use WebSocket for price updates and place orders via REST (or via native client).  
  - *Polymarket:* Polymarket offers REST/WebSocket “Gamma” and “CLOB” APIs, plus open SDKs in Python/TypeScript【36†L15-L18】【37†L45-L53】. Bots interact with Polymarket’s on-chain orderbook (a central limit book on Polygon). Orders must be EIP-712 signed, but the SDK handles this. Because Polymarket 5‑min markets move in milliseconds, efficient data access is crucial: practitioners moved from REST polling to WebSockets to slash latency from seconds to milliseconds【29†L121-L130】.  
- **Execution logic:** Your bot’s core loop typically *listens* to live data, applies a signal function, then issues orders. Real-time feeds (especially via WebSockets) minimize latency. For fast markets like Polymarket or high-frequency crypto, compiled languages (Go, Rust) or async frameworks can reduce delays【29†L121-L130】. After sending an order, the bot should monitor fills and may need to cancel or adjust if only partially filled.  

## Position Management and Exits  
Bots need clear exit rules to close positions:  
- **Profit targets and time stops:** Set explicit profit-taking levels or fixed timeouts. For example, close a stock position after a 5% gain or at end-of-day; close a crypto swing trade after 1 hour. Polymarket positions close automatically at resolution, but some bots preemptively unwind if a target probability is reached.  
- **Stop-loss orders:** As a risk guard, attach stop-loss orders to auto-close losing trades. Nurp’s risk guide notes *fixed stops* (constant price gap), *trailing stops* (lock in gains as price moves), and *ATR-based stops* (distance based on volatility)【33†L170-L177】. For instance, you could program an LLM-informed strategy to enter a trade and simultaneously place a stop-loss a few ATRs below entry【33†L170-L177】. Trailing stops are useful in strongly trending markets to maximize profit runs.  
- **Technical exit signals:** Use technical charts/LLM signals for exit. An LLM might generate a rationale like “price has pierced the 50-day MA”, cueing an exit. Or use chart patterns (support/resistance) coded into the system. Incorporating multiple exit criteria (time, profit, technical, news reversals) tends to be safest.  
- **Polymarket specifics:** Polymarket contracts settle at $1 or $0. The bot effectively “closes” when the event resolves. Strategies often focus on getting positions before resolution. Active market-making bots simply cancel all outstanding orders at resolution.  
- **Execution of exits:** The bot should verify fill status and possibly switch to more aggressive orders if a target/stop won’t fill. Logging and monitoring ensure you know when a position is fully closed.  

## Risk Management and Capital Controls  
Safeguarding capital is vital. Key practices include【31†L36-L44】【33†L170-L177】:  
- **Position sizing:** Limit risk per trade (e.g. 1–2% of total capital). Use formulas to translate a desired risk amount (with stop-loss distance) into a position size【31†L36-L44】. For example, risking 1% of \$100k (\$1k) with a \$10 stop-loss implies buying 100 shares【31†L79-L87】. Adjust sizes for volatility: smaller sizes when the market swings more【33†L293-L300】.  
- **Diversification:** Don’t concentrate all capital in one asset or highly correlated bets【31†L37-L44】. Spread strategies across sectors/coins or mix stock/crypto portfolios. In crypto, this may mean trading several uncorrelated tokens. In prediction markets, it could mean limiting exposure to just a few events at a time.  
- **Stop-loss and drawdown rules:** As above, use stop-loss orders to cap single-trade losses【33†L170-L177】. Also set **maximum drawdown** limits on the portfolio (e.g. pause trading if total equity drops 5%)【33†L225-L234】. Nurp suggests auto-halting trading at a 5% drawdown threshold and re-evaluating strategy【33†L225-L234】. Automated alerts or shutdowns can enforce these limits.  
- **Risk matrices and scoring:** It can help to classify trades by risk level. For instance, a *risk matrix* (Probability vs Impact) can map trades into “Acceptable” or “Unacceptable” zones. If a trade has both high uncertainty and high potential loss, the system would label it “Unacceptable” and skip it. (See example risk chart below.) This formalizes risk appetite for each strategy.

- **Volatility adjustment:** In volatile markets (like crypto), widen stops or reduce position sizes. For example, use ATR to scale stops and size – e.g. set stop = 3×ATR away and adjust size so that this risk = 1% of capital【33†L170-L177】【33†L184-L193】.  
- **Monitoring and failsafes:** Continuously track bot health. Limit the number of simultaneous positions or open orders. Implement “kill switches”: e.g. if too many stop-losses are hit in a row, pause trading. Use real-time dashboards or logs to catch anomalies.  
- **LLM-specific caution:** LLMs can hallucinate or be misled by bad data (see the “adversarial news” study【9†L105-L113】). Always guard LLM outputs with logic rules. For instance, if an LLM’s trade recommendation contradicts fundamental limits (e.g. “buy 1000 shares if insufficient funds”), flag it. Consider vetting critical inputs with a secondary model or historical check.  

## Backtesting Best Practices  
Thorough backtesting is essential before going live. Key steps:  
- **Historical data quality:** Use clean, timestamped data including prices, volumes, and (if relevant) news or social signals. For stocks, use minute or tick data if your strategy is intraday. Include realistic *transaction costs* (commissions, bid-ask spreads) and *slippage*. Crypto exchanges have fees and often wide spreads – model those costs. Polymarket betting has its own fees/market rules (account for any trading fee or spread to the AMM).  
- **Simulate orders realistically:** Emulate how your orders would fill. If backtesting a limit order strategy, assume partial fills or no-fill if the price never reached. For aggressive market orders, factor in worst-case slippage. This avoids the “optimistic fill” bias.  
- **Walk-forward and out-of-sample:** Don’t just optimize on one period. Split data into train/test or use a walk-forward (rolling window) approach. For example, calibrate on one year of data, then test on the next quarter, and iterate. This helps detect overfitting – avoid a strategy that only worked on a specific historical context【35†L142-L151】.  
- **Paper review of trades:** Log each hypothetical trade during backtest with reasons. Review manually to ensure the bot logic actually caught the intended signals【29†L148-L154】. One Polymarket bot writer saved all generated trade signals and manually inspected them before live use to catch logic bugs【29†L148-L154】.  
- **Backtesting tools:** Use established frameworks when possible. Python’s Backtrader, Zipline, Backtesting.py, or QuantConnect’s Lean allow strategy coding with built-in simulators. For Polymarket, you may replay historical order book snapshots if available; e.g. Frosty_Expression’s Polymarket bot logged past 5-minute markets to validate strategies【29†L148-L154】.  

Backtesting reveals strengths and weaknesses: you can *tune* risk controls (like adjusting stop levels) by observing historical drawdowns【35†L112-L121】. It also uncovers hidden assumptions: for example, backtests may warn if a strategy always “wins” only during certain rare market patterns. Always cross-check that your bot wouldn’t have violated exchange rules (like margin limits) in the backtest.  

## Simulated (Paper) Trading  
After backtesting, run the bot in a **paper-trading environment** to validate it on live data without real money【35†L39-L42】【35†L59-L67】.  
- **Set up a live feed:** Connect to real-time market data streams (stock tickers or order books) but direct the bot’s orders to a paper account or simulator. Alpaca offers paper accounts for equities; Binance and others have testnets or “dry-run” modes for crypto. Polymarket may allow simulating trades on a fork or sandbox if available, or you can mirror the live API calls without sending on-chain orders.  
- **Monitor behavior:** Check that the bot’s positions and P/L behave sensibly. Paper trading exposes issues like slower fills or more frequent stop-outs than backtests predicted【35†L81-L90】. It also shows how the bot handles unexpected situations (e.g. API downtime, partial fills, Internet lag).  
- **Refine based on feedback:** If paper trading reveals too many false triggers or missed fills, adjust the logic. For example, an LLM-triggered “signal” might need a confirmation by a technical filter. The QuantInsti example notes that live-simulated performance in volatile conditions (e.g. Fed announcements) often leads to strategy tweaks like adding volatility filters【35†L73-L81】.  
- **Limitations:** Paper trading can’t fully mimic all slippage or liquidity impact【35†L81-L90】. A strategy that seems fine on paper might still trigger larger losses live when orders actually move the market. So keep initial live sizes small.  

Using both methods (backtesting + paper) is recommended: backtesting checks historical edge, paper tests robustness to current market quirks【35†L39-L42】. The article on crypto bots advises never to skip these steps before live deployment【35†L33-L42】.  

## Market-Specific Considerations  
- **Equity Markets:** Stocks trade on exchanges with defined hours and regulations. Market data (open/high/low/close) is widely available. News and fundamentals (earnings, SEC filings) move stocks; LLMs can parse SEC filings or newswires for signals. Equities allow many order types (stop-limit, trailing stops, etc.) via broker APIs. Liquidity in large-cap stocks is deep, but be cautious with thinly traded small caps. Brokerage rules (margin, pattern-day-trader rules) can affect bot design.  
- **Prediction Markets (Polymarket):** These are *event-based* with binary outcome tokens (pay $1 if event occurs). Liquidity comes from other traders and on-chain liquidity; Polymarket’s current CLOB setup means bots can post or take limit orders【39†L25-L30】. Bets settle on Polygon via an oracle (e.g. UMA). Markets are short-lived (minutes to days). Key differences: the “price” is a probability (e.g. a 0.8 price = 80% chance). Bots often use very short timeframes (like 5-minute Bitcoin markets)【29†L107-L115】. In such fast markets, latency is critical – as noted, using WebSockets and optimized code is essential【29†L121-L130】. Unique risks include oracle disputes (rare) and gas/token issues, but otherwise it’s similar to crypto trading on speed.  
- **Cryptocurrency Markets:** Crypto trades 24/7, so bots can run non-stop (ideally on reliable servers). Exchanges vary in liquidity – Bitcoin and Ether are deep, many altcoins are thin. Crypto also allows decentralized exchanges (AMMs) where liquidity pools can be traded via smart contracts; LLMs might integrate on-chain signals (whale transfers, DeFi metrics). Volatility is generally higher, so risk controls (wide stops, small sizes) are even more crucial. On the plus side, crypto often has well-documented REST/WebSocket APIs (Binance, Coinbase Pro, etc.) and fewer trading restrictions (though beware exchange-specific rules). Funding rates and perpetual swaps are unique: if using leverage, the bot must track funding payments. LLMs here can incorporate on-chain news (e.g. GitHub commits, tweet sentiment), but remember the “adversarial news” risk in any market【9†L87-L94】.
